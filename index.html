What AI actually is — and isn't

  1. 1
    AI is a reasoning engine, not a search engine.
    It generates — doesn't retrieve. It can be wrong. Confidently wrong. Verification stays with you, always.
  2. 2
    Your prompt is your lever. Everything else is commentary.
    A 3-word prompt gives you a generic intern. A structured prompt gives you a great analyst.
  3. 3
    Treat it like a smart new hire who needs a proper brief.
    Role, context, task, format. Give all four and you'll be amazed. Give one and you'll be disappointed.
  4. 4
    People who get the most from AI iterate fast.
    First output is a draft. Run corrections. Tighten again. Three passes beats one "perfect" prompt every time.

See a real worked example ▼
Problem: Every Monday I spend 2 hours pulling last week's sales data from three systems and writing a summary email for my VP.

Weak prompt: "Summarize last week's sales."
Output: A generic paragraph with no structure, no variance analysis, no action items. Would need complete rewrite.

Strong prompt: "You are a sales analyst. Here is last week's data by region [paste]. Write a 150-word summary for a VP. Structure: 1) headline number vs target, 2) top 2 regions and why, 3) one risk to flag. Bullet points only."
Output: Structured, send-ready in one pass. Time: 8 minutes instead of 2 hours.

ROI: 1hr 52min saved. Every week.

The RCTFX framework

  1. R
    Role — tell it who to be
    "You are a senior operations analyst with 15 years in manufacturing."
  2. C
    Context — give it the situation
    Business problem, audience, decisions this will inform.
  3. T
    Task — be explicit with verbs
    Write / Summarize / Analyze / Draft / Critique / Compare.
  4. F
    Format — tell it how to structure output
    Bullets, table, paragraphs, length limit.
  5. X
    Constraints — what to exclude
    "No jargon. Under 150 words. Don't recommend budget increases."
See RCTFX applied to a real problem ▼
R: You are a supply chain risk analyst.
C: We have 3 tier-1 suppliers in Southeast Asia and one has flagged a potential 4-week delay due to port congestion. Our Q3 launch depends on their component.
T: Draft a risk assessment memo for our VP of Operations.
F: One page max. Sections: situation summary, risk rating (High/Med/Low with reason), 3 mitigation options with tradeoffs, recommended action.
X: No technical jargon. Assume the reader knows the business but not the supplier relationship details.

Result: Send-ready memo in 6 minutes. Previously took half a day to draft, circulate, and align.

Prompt patterns by function

FinanceYou are a financial analyst. Given this data [paste], identify the top 3 variances from plan. For each: state the variance, the likely driver, and one corrective action. Format as a table.
Supply chainYou are a supply chain risk analyst. Review this supplier update [paste]. Flag delivery, quality, or compliance risks. Rate each High/Medium/Low with one sentence of reasoning. Priority order.
StrategyYou are a strategy consultant. Summarize this competitive intelligence [paste] into a 3-part exec narrative: market shift → implications for us → recommended response. One paragraph each.
Operations / ManufacturingYou are a process improvement expert. Here is our current process [paste]. Identify the 3 biggest bottlenecks. For each: name it, explain impact, suggest one AI-enabled fix.

When AI gets it wrong — and how to catch it

Hallucination

Invents facts or numbers that sound plausible.

Fix: Verify figures against source data before using in exec reports.

Overconfidence

Answers when it should say "I don't know."

Fix: Add "Note anywhere you're uncertain" to your prompt.

Generic output

Gives you a template when you needed a judgment.

Fix: Add more specific context and constraints, then re-run.

Sycophancy

Agrees with you even when you're wrong.

Fix: Ask it to steelman the opposite view before agreeing.

Which AI tool for which job?

Confidential or sensitive data?
Yes → Company-approved enterprise tool only. Never paste sensitive data into consumer AI.
No → Continue below.
Writing, summarizing, or reasoning?
Yes → ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini all work well.
No → Continue below.
Need current info or web search?
Yes → Perplexity, ChatGPT with search, or Gemini with Google.
No → Continue below.
Need to build or code something?
Yes → Claude or ChatGPT. See Session 4 for the full builder track.
No → Any major tool. Pick one and get good at it before diversifying.

    Before you go further

    Guardrails

    AI is powerful. Power without judgment creates risk. Non-negotiable.

    The one rule that covers everything

    If you're unsure whether something should go into an AI tool — it probably shouldn't. Describe the problem without the sensitive details. You can get 90% of the value without giving AI your confidential data.

    Never paste confidential data, customer PII, or unreleased financials into consumer AI tools.
    Describe the structure, not the content.
    Verify before you use. AI outputs are drafts, not decisions.
    Any figure in an exec report must be checked against source data.
    AI is a thought partner. You are the decision maker.
    Judgment, authority, and accountability stay with you.
    Build trust with AI before you rely on it.
    Low-stakes tasks first. Understand failure modes before going critical.
