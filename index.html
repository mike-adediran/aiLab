What AI actually is — and isn't

  1. 1
    AI is a reasoning engine, not a search engine.
    It generates — doesn't retrieve. It can be wrong. Confidently wrong. Verification stays with you, always.
  2. 2
    Your prompt is your lever. Everything else is commentary.
    A 3-word prompt gives you a generic intern. A structured prompt gives you a great analyst.
  3. 3
    Treat it like a smart new hire who needs a proper brief.
    Role, context, task, format. Give all four and you'll be amazed. Give one and you'll be disappointed.
  4. 4
    People who get the most from AI iterate fast.
    First output is a draft. Run corrections. Tighten again. Three passes beats one "perfect" prompt every time.

The RCTFX framework — how to talk to AI

Every great AI output starts with a structured prompt. RCTFX gives you five levers. Use all five and you will be amazed. Use one and you will be disappointed.

  1. R
    Role — tell it who to be
    "You are a senior supply chain analyst with 10 years in manufacturing." Sets tone, depth, and vocabulary instantly.
  2. C
    Context — give it the situation
    What is the business problem? Who is the audience? What decision will this inform? The more relevant context, the less generic the output.
  3. T
    Task — be explicit with verbs
    Write / Summarize / Analyze / Draft / Critique / Compare. "Help me with" is weak. "Draft a 150-word summary for..." is strong.
  4. F
    Format — tell it how to structure the output
    Bullets, table, paragraphs, length limit. Without this it guesses — often wrong.
  5. X
    Constraints — what to exclude
    "No jargon. Under 150 words. Don't recommend budget increases." Negative instructions are underused and very effective.
See RCTFX applied to a real problem ▼
R: You are a supply chain risk analyst.

C: We have 3 tier-1 suppliers in Southeast Asia. One has flagged a potential 4-week delay due to port congestion. Our Q3 launch depends on their component.

T: Draft a risk assessment memo for our VP of Operations.

F: One page max. Sections: situation summary, risk rating (High/Med/Low with reason), 3 mitigation options with tradeoffs, recommended action.

X: No technical jargon. Assume the reader knows the business but not the supplier relationship details.

Result: Send-ready memo in 6 minutes. Previously took half a day to draft, circulate, and align.

What's missing?
— Role: Who should the AI be?
— Context: What is "this"? Why does the manager need it?
— Task: Summary of what — key decisions? Risks? Next steps?
— Format: Email? Bullet points? One paragraph?
— Constraints: How long? What to exclude?

When AI gets it wrong — and how to catch it

Hallucination

Invents facts or numbers that sound plausible.

Fix: Verify figures against source data before using in exec reports.

Overconfidence

Answers when it should say "I don't know."

Fix: Add "Note anywhere you're uncertain" to your prompt.

Generic output

Gives you a template when you needed a judgment.

Fix: Add more specific context and constraints, then re-run.

Sycophancy

Agrees with you even when you're wrong.

Fix: Ask it to steelman the opposite view before agreeing.

Output quality check

Speed without quality is just fast mediocrity. Before you present any AI output, run it through these four questions.

Is it accurate?

Verify every fact, figure, and claim against source data. AI sounds confident even when wrong.

Is it complete?

Did it address everything you asked? Missing steps or criteria are common failure modes.

Does it sound like you?

Generic output is a signal the prompt lacked context. Add your voice before sending.

Would you sign your name to it?

If you would not put your name on it as-is, it is not ready. Edit until you would.

Before you go further

Guardrails

AI is powerful. Power without judgment creates risk. Non-negotiable.

The one rule that covers everything

If you're unsure whether something should go into an AI tool — it probably shouldn't. Describe the problem without the sensitive details. You can get 90% of the value without giving AI your confidential data.

Never paste confidential data, customer PII, or unreleased financials into consumer AI tools.
Describe the structure, not the content.
Verify before you use. AI outputs are drafts, not decisions.
Any figure in an exec report must be checked against source data.
AI is a thought partner. You are the decision maker.
Judgment, authority, and accountability stay with you.
Build trust with AI before you rely on it.
Low-stakes tasks first. Understand failure modes before going critical.
